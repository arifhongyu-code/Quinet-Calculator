Quick Reference: Hourly to Salary Conversion

Use the formula below to convert hourly wages to different pay periods. All calculations assume your base hourly rate without overtime.

Annual Salary = Hourly Rate × Hours/Week × Weeks/Year
Monthly = Annual ÷ 12
Weekly = Hourly Rate × Hours/Week
Daily = Hourly Rate × (Hours/Week ÷ 5)

How to Calculate Hourly to Salary Conversion

Converting your hourly wage to an annual salary helps you understand your true earning potential and compare job offers. The calculation is straightforward: multiply your hourly rate by the number of hours you work per week, then multiply by the number of weeks you work per year.

Full-Time vs. Part-Time Hours

In the United States, full-time employment is typically defined as 40 hours per week. Part-time positions may range from 10 to 30 hours per week. Some positions require more than 40 hours (salaried positions or overtime work). When calculating your income, use your average weekly hours, including any regular overtime.

Standard Working Year

A standard year has 52 weeks of work. However, most employees receive paid time off (PTO) including vacation days and company holidays. If you have 2 weeks of vacation plus 10 holidays (which equals roughly 3 weeks off), you would work approximately 49 weeks per year. Adjust the weeks calculation based on your actual paid time off.

Understanding Gross vs. Net Income

Your gross income is your total earnings before taxes and deductions. This is what this calculator primarily shows you. Your net income (take-home pay) is what remains after federal income tax, Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%), and other deductions. Typically, employees take home 70–80% of their gross salary, depending on their tax bracket and deductions.

Overtime and Bonuses

If you regularly work overtime (hours over 40 per week), most employers pay 1.5 times your hourly rate (time-and-a-half). To include overtime in your calculation, estimate your average weekly hours including overtime, or calculate the overtime separately. Bonuses and commissions should be added separately to your annual salary estimate.
