How US Federal Income Tax Works in 2025

The United States uses a progressive federal income tax system, meaning different portions of your income are taxed at different rates. You only pay the higher rate on the income that falls within that bracket — not on your entire income.

Standard Deduction for 2025

Before calculating your tax, you subtract either the standard deduction or your itemized deductions from your gross income. For 2025, the standard deduction is $15,000 for single filers, $30,000 for married filing jointly, and $22,500 for head of household. Most taxpayers use the standard deduction as it is larger than their itemized expenses.

FICA Taxes — Social Security & Medicare

In addition to federal income tax, most employees pay FICA taxes. Social Security is 6.2% on wages up to $176,100 (the 2025 wage base). Medicare is 1.45% on all wages, plus an additional 0.9% on wages above $200,000 for single filers ($250,000 for married filing jointly).

401(k) and Pre-Tax Contributions

Contributing to a traditional 401(k) reduces your federal taxable income. For 2025, the employee contribution limit is $23,500 ($31,000 if age 50 or older). This can significantly lower your tax bill — someone in the 22% bracket who contributes $10,000 saves $2,200 in federal taxes.

Your marginal rate is the tax rate on your last dollar of income — your highest bracket. Your effective rate is the average rate across all your income. Because the US tax system is progressive, your effective rate is always lower than your marginal rate. For example, a single filer earning $75,000 has a 22% marginal rate but typically pays an effective rate of around 13–15%.
